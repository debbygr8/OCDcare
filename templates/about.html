What is OCD?

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a mental illness characterized by intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors (compulsions).

Examples of obsessions include fear of contamination, disturbing thoughts, or worry about safety. Compulsions may involve excessive handwashing, checking, or arranging things repeatedly to reduce anxiety.

While many people may have mild obsessive habits, OCD becomes a disorder when these behaviors interfere with daily life and cause distress.

Common Symptoms of OCD

Disturbing intrusive thoughts

Individuals with OCD often experience unwanted, intrusive thoughts that are distressing and difficult to control. These thoughts may be violent, sexual, or blasphemous in nature, causing significant shame and anxiety. The individual may try to suppress or neutralize these thoughts through compulsive behaviors.

Fear of germs or contamination

Individuals with this type of OCD have an intense fear of becoming contaminated by germs, dirt, or other perceived contaminants. They may obsessively clean, wash their hands, or avoid touching certain objects to prevent the spread of contamination. This can lead to disruptions in daily life and significant distress.

Need for symmetry, order, or perfection

People with this symptom feel compelled to repeatedly check things like locks, appliances, or light switches to ensure they are properly secured. This is driven by the fear that something bad will happen if they don't check these items over and over. Checking behaviors can become extremely time-consuming and interfere with normal activities.

Compulsions like counting, touching, or repeating actions

OCD can manifest in the form of repetitive behaviors or mental rituals that the individual feels they must perform. This may include counting, tapping, touching objects in a specific order, or repeating actions a certain number of times. These compulsions are aimed at reducing anxiety or preventing a perceived negative outcome, but they provide only temporary relief.
